Quantitative Research Analyst

## Long write-up

I joined Greenland in November 2023 because they were the only firm who'd take me. As an intern. Imagine being out of college and still being an intern. It kinda sucked as far as identity and self-esteem is concerned. But the office was sick and I got to take Cityflo to and fro. My routine was impeccable. Wake up, Go to the gym, get ready, have breakfast, catch the bus, solve nyt puzzles, read a book, get to work, read pricing theory, implement it in code, do other side-quests for the rest of the team. Optimize existing code. Catch the bus back home. Read a book. Get home, have dinner, sleep, repeat.

I had no time to think. I was just executing like a robot. I never felt existential or self-reflective. Every part of my day was planned out. I dated on the weekends. The robot life was great.

Gold Quanto Pricing Theory is actually pretty cool math. I think I spent too much time on it but building on mathematical foundations with rigour doesn't go to waste. People will apply pressure on you to go faster but identify that it's just a game. They assigned you to do something because they can't do it themselves. That's all the leverage you need. 

I learnt how to use Excel. Excel is good if you're a grandpa, honestly if you still use Excel for data analysis, just quit and re-evaluate. Write in english make an analysis dashboard on this data using python or javascript and give it to claude. 

I worked with a lot of Bloomberg too. Bloomberg is also old news. Pulling any data is a one prompt shot. Wasted skill. Good thing I don't remember any Bloomberg anymore. 

I also got to work on Chinese Index ETFs. Lots of data pulling, data cleaning, corporate event price adjusting. All of this is probably one-shottable now but I don't expect the people at Greenland to be AI-native yet. It takes a lot for an elephant code-base to look inwards and come to the conclusion that this is probably a bad way to do things. I'd love to be proved wrong though.

I learnt lots about Fair Value and EV based strategy thinking here. First principle pricing, trading the deviations. Usually works well in priceable things like commodities. Not so much in equities. There you have to be creative to the extent that other people just don't get it, I think.

Anyway, I converted the internship to a full time position in a month. The power games were humourous. They tried boxing me in with a "Write 10 things you did that created an impact then we'll give you an offer." Imagine that, 22 working days and write 10 things you did that created an impact. I had a sense that there will be such power games so I had a log of everything I did that was impactful, with human witnesses for each point to corroborate. Showed up 5 minutes later with the writeup. My manager stifled his surprise, I grinned. Please don't mess with me.

The place was a parking spot anyway. Who wants to trade commodity spreads, bro? 6-12% annualized? This was too boring to be sustainable. I worked on getting another offer, got one, lied that I want to study further and expressed my desire to quit. They deleted my system before I got the last word in. Egos were hurt. I walked out.
